# Sparsest Cut and Metric Embeddings

The sparsest cut problem and its connection to metric embeddings reveals deep relationships between graph algorithms and geometry.

## Sparsest Cut

The **sparsest cut** of a graph $G = (V, E)$ is:
$$\Phi(G) = \min_{S \subset V} \frac{|\{(u,v) \in E : u \in S, v \notin S\}|}{|S| \cdot |V \setminus S|}$$

This measures edge expansion normalized by the product of set sizes. The ARV algorithm (Arora-Rao-Vazirani) gives an $O(\sqrt{\log n})$-approximation.

## Metric Embeddings

**Bourgain's Theorem:** Any $n$-point metric space embeds into $\ell_1$ with distortion $O(\log n)$. This is tight: some metrics (e.g., expander graphs) require $\Omega(\log n)$ distortion.

<checkyourself>
Describe Bourgain's embedding construction at a high level. How does it achieve $O(\log n)$ distortion?

<showhide>
Bourgain's construction: For each scale $k = 0, 1, \ldots, \log n$ and each of $O(\log n)$ random subsets $S_{k,i}$ at scale $k$ (each vertex included with probability $1/2^k$): assign coordinate $(k, i)$ of point $v$ to be $d(v, S_{k,i})$ = distance to nearest point in $S_{k,i}$. The embedding has $O(\log^2 n)$ dimensions. Nearby points (distance $r$) map to nearby coordinates (with high probability, their distances to the same sets are similar). Far-apart points are separated because at the right scale, one of the random sets contains one point but not the other. The analysis shows distortion $O(\log n)$.
</showhide>
