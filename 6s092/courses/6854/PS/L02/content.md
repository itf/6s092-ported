# Balls into Bins / Power of Two Choices

Throwing $n$ balls uniformly at random into $n$ bins models load balancing. The maximum load concentrates sharply around $\Theta(\log n / \log \log n)$ with high probability.

## Maximum Load Analysis

With $n$ balls in $n$ bins, the probability that a specific bin receives $k$ or more balls is $\binom{n}{k}(1/n)^k \leq (e/k)^k$. Setting this $\leq 1/n^2$ gives $k = \Theta(\log n / \log \log n)$.

<checkyourself>
Give a high-level argument for why the maximum load is $\Theta(\log n / \log \log n)$ and not simply $O(\log n)$.

<showhide>
The birthday paradox intuition: with $\log n / \log \log n$ balls in a bin, the probability of that event is roughly $1/n^2$. Taking a union bound over $n$ bins gives total failure probability $O(1/n)$. The $\log\log n$ denominator comes from optimizing the Poisson approximation—the threshold $k$ satisfies $(e/k)^k = 1/n^2$, giving $k \ln(e/k) = 2\ln n$, so $k \approx \frac{\log n}{\log \log n}$.
</showhide>
</checkyourself>

## Power of Two Choices

Instead of placing each ball in a random bin, we sample **two** random bins and place the ball in the less-loaded one. This simple change reduces the maximum load from $\Theta(\log n / \log \log n)$ to $\Theta(\log \log n)$—an exponential improvement.

<checkyourself>
Why does adding a **third** choice (pick 3 bins, place in the least loaded) not give an asymptotically better bound than two choices?

<showhide>
The maximum load with $d$ choices is $\frac{\ln \ln n}{\ln d} + O(1)$ w.h.p. Going from $d=1$ to $d=2$ drops the load from $\frac{\ln n}{\ln \ln n}$ to $\frac{\ln \ln n}{\ln 2}$—an exponential improvement. Going from $d=2$ to $d=3$ only reduces it by a constant factor $(\ln 3 / \ln 2 \approx 1.58)$, so two choices already captures essentially all the gain.
</showhide>
</checkyourself>
