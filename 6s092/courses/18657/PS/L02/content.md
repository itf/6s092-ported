# Lecture 2: Binary Classification and the Bayes Classifier

This lecture formalizes binary classification and introduces the **Bayes classifier**, the optimal decision rule given perfect knowledge of the conditional distribution $P_{Y|X}$. We derive the Bayes risk and show that the excess risk of any classifier can be expressed in terms of the regression function $\eta(X) = \mathbb{P}(Y=1|X)$.

## The Bayes Classifier

Given $\eta(X) = \mathbb{P}(Y=1|X)$, the Bayes classifier $h^*$ is:
$$h^*(x) = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } \eta(x) > 1/2 \\ 0 & \text{if } \eta(x) \leq 1/2 \end{cases}$$

This classifier predicts whichever label is more probable given $X = x$.

<checkyourself>
Why is the Bayes classifier called "optimal"? What does it minimize?

<showhide>
The Bayes classifier minimizes the classification error $R(h) = \mathbb{P}(h(X) \neq Y)$ over all possible classifiers $h$. Its risk $R^* = R(h^*)$ is the smallest achievable risk, called the Bayes risk.
</showhide>
</checkyourself>

## Bayes Risk Formula

The Bayes risk satisfies:
$$R(h^*) = \mathbb{E}[\min(\eta(X), 1-\eta(X))] \leq \frac{1}{2}.$$

Moreover, for any classifier $h$:
$$R(h) - R(h^*) = \mathbb{E}_X[|2\eta(X) - 1| \cdot \mathbf{1}(h(X) \neq h^*(X))].$$

## Excess Risk Decomposition

A key identity is that the excess risk (regret) of any classifier $h$ over the Bayes classifier is:
$$R(h) - R(h^*) = \mathbb{E}_X\bigl[|2\eta(X)-1| \cdot \mathbf{1}(h(X) \neq h^*(X))\bigr] \geq 0.$$

This shows that $h$ only incurs excess risk at points where it disagrees with $h^*$, and the penalty is proportional to $|2\eta(x)-1|$, how "confident" the Bayes classifier is at that point.

<checkyourself>
What does the term $|2\eta(x) - 1|$ measure? Why is it relevant to the cost of a mistake at point $x$?

<showhide>
$|2\eta(x)-1|$ measures how far the regression function is from $1/2$ at $x$. When $\eta(x)$ is close to $1/2$, the labels are nearly equally likely and a mistake at $x$ costs little. When $\eta(x)$ is far from $1/2$ (close to 0 or 1), the correct label is highly certain, and a mistake is more costly.
</showhide>
