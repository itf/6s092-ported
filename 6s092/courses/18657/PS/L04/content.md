# Lecture 4: Fast Rates and Noise Conditions

This lecture studies how *noise conditions* on the regression function $\eta(X)$ lead to faster convergence rates for the empirical risk minimizer. Without noise conditions, the estimation error scales as $O(n^{-1/2})$. Under **Massart's noise condition**, we can achieve the fast rate $O(n^{-1})$.

## Noise in Binary Classification

Noise in binary classification means $\eta(X)$ is close to $1/2$: the labels are uncertain. The excess risk of the ERM is:
$$\hat{E}(\hat{h}) = R(\hat{h}) - R(h^*)$$
We want to bound this quantity.

## Massart's Noise Condition

**Definition:** The noise satisfies **Massart's condition** with constant $\gamma \in (0, 1/2]$ if:
$$|\eta(X) - 1/2| \geq \gamma \quad \text{almost surely}.$$

This means the regression function is bounded away from $1/2$: the labels are never too uncertain.

<checkyourself>
What does Massart's noise condition with $\gamma = 1/2$ correspond to?

<showhide>
$\gamma = 1/2$ means $|\eta(X) - 1/2| \geq 1/2$, so $\eta(X) \in \{0, 1\}$ almost surely. This is the noiseless case: the label is deterministically determined by the features.
</showhide>
</checkyourself>

## Fast Rate Theorem

**Theorem:** Under Massart's noise condition with constant $\gamma$, the ERM $\hat{h}$ over a finite class $|\mathcal{H}| = M$ satisfies, with probability at least $1 - \delta$:
$$R(\hat{h}) - R(h^*) \leq \frac{\log(M/\delta)}{\gamma n}.$$

This is a rate of $O(1/n)$ rather than $O(1/\sqrt{n})$: the **fast rate**.

## Bernstein's Inequality

The fast rate proof uses **Bernstein's inequality** rather than Hoeffding's. Bernstein exploits the *variance* of the summands: if $|Z_i| \leq b$ and $\text{Var}(Z_i) \leq \sigma^2$, then:
$$\mathbb{P}\left(\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (Z_i - \mathbb{E}Z_i) \geq t\right) \leq \exp\left(\frac{-nt^2/2}{\sigma^2 + bt/3}\right).$$

Under Massart's condition, the variance of the loss differences is small, enabling the fast rate.
