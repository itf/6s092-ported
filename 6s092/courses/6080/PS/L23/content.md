# Quantum Computing (continued)

The second part of the quantum computing lectures covers **multi-qubit systems**, **quantum entanglement**, and the **complexity class BQP**. For $n$ qubits, the state space is $\mathbb{C}^{2^n}$: exponentially large. Entangled states like the **Bell state** $\frac{|00\rangle + |11\rangle}{\sqrt{2}}$ cannot be written as a tensor product of individual qubit states. Measuring one qubit of an entangled pair instantly determines the other's outcome — not because of faster-than-light communication (no information is transmitted), but because of correlations built into the joint state.

**BQP** (Bounded-error Quantum Polynomial time) is the quantum analogue of BPP. It contains problems solvable by a quantum computer in polynomial time with error $\leq 1/3$. Known relations: $P \subseteq BPP \subseteq BQP \subseteq PSPACE$. Whether $BQP \subseteq NP$ or $NP \subseteq BQP$ is unknown; Shor's factoring algorithm shows factoring $\in BQP$, but factoring is not known to be in NP-complete or to be NP-hard.

## Entanglement and Bell States

The Bell state $|\Phi^+\rangle = \frac{|00\rangle + |11\rangle}{\sqrt{2}}$ is maximally entangled: measuring the first qubit in the standard basis collapses the state — if you see 0, the second qubit is also 0; if you see 1, the second is also 1. This perfect correlation exists regardless of the spatial separation of the qubits, and is what Einstein called "spooky action at a distance." Bell's theorem (1964) proves these correlations cannot be explained by any local hidden variable theory.

## The Complexity Class BQP

BQP is defined analogously to BPP: problems solvable by a uniform family of polynomial-size quantum circuits, with error $\leq 1/3$. Known results: $P \subseteq BQP$ (any deterministic circuit is a valid quantum circuit), $BQP \subseteq PSPACE$ (quantum states can be simulated with polynomial space). Factoring and discrete log are in BQP (Shor), unstructured database search has a quadratic speedup (Grover). It is widely believed $BQP \not\supseteq NP$.

<checkyourself>
Why does a quantum computer with $n$ qubits have an exponentially large state space, but this does not mean it can solve all exponential-time problems?

<showhide>
The state of $n$ qubits lives in $\mathbb{C}^{2^n}$, but you cannot access all $2^n$ amplitudes directly — measurement collapses the state to a single basis state. The art of quantum algorithms is to design circuits that amplify the amplitude of the correct answer while canceling amplitudes of wrong answers, so a single measurement (or few measurements) yields the answer with high probability. Simply having a superposition over all inputs doesn't automatically solve hard problems.
