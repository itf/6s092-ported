# Quantum Computing

A **qubit** is the basic unit of quantum information. Unlike a classical bit (0 or 1), a qubit can be in a *superposition* $\alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle$ where $\alpha, \beta \in \mathbb{C}$ and $|\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1$. When measured in the standard basis, the qubit collapses to $|0\rangle$ with probability $|\alpha|^2$ and $|1\rangle$ with probability $|\beta|^2$. The state of $n$ qubits is a unit vector in $\mathbb{C}^{2^n}$ — exponentially large.

Quantum operations other than measurement are **unitary transformations**: linear maps $U$ with $UU^\dagger = I$. Unitary transformations are reversible, deterministic, and continuous. Key single-qubit gates: the **Hadamard** $H = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\1&-1\end{pmatrix}$ (creates uniform superposition), the **NOT** gate $X = \begin{pmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}$, and the **phase** gate. The "square root of NOT" is $H$ applied twice... applied with a phase correction.

## Quantum States and Superposition

The Hadamard gate transforms $|0\rangle \to \frac{|0\rangle + |1\rangle}{\sqrt{2}} = |+\rangle$ and $|1\rangle \to \frac{|0\rangle - |1\rangle}{\sqrt{2}} = |-\rangle$. Applied to $|0\rangle^{\otimes n}$, it creates the uniform superposition over all $2^n$ computational basis states: $H^{\otimes n}|0\rangle^{\otimes n} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2^n}}\sum_{x \in \{0,1\}^n} |x\rangle$.

## Unitary Gates and Quantum Circuits

A **quantum circuit** is a sequence of unitary gates applied to qubits, followed by measurements. Common two-qubit gates include the **CNOT** (controlled-NOT): $|x,y\rangle \to |x, y \oplus x\rangle$. The **Toffoli gate** (CCNOT) is universal for classical reversible computation: $|a,b,c\rangle \to |a,b,c \oplus (a \wedge b)\rangle$. The combination $\{H, \text{CNOT}, T\}$ is universal for quantum computation.

<checkyourself>
Why must quantum operations (other than measurement) be unitary? What would happen if we applied a non-unitary matrix to a quantum state?

<showhide>
Quantum states must have unit norm (probabilities sum to 1). A matrix $U$ preserves unit norm for all vectors iff $UU^\dagger = I$ — i.e., $U$ is unitary. If we applied a non-unitary matrix, the resulting "state" might have norm $\neq 1$, which would mean probabilities don't sum to 1. Physically, this corresponds to the Schrödinger equation, which generates unitary time evolution.
