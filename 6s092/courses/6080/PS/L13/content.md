# Randomness

Probability is notoriously counter-intuitive. This lecture sharpens intuition through classic puzzles (two children, three prisoners, Monty Hall) before introducing the formal computational role of randomness. The Monty Hall problem reveals the importance of *modeling assumptions*: whether the host knows where the car is and always opens a goat door changes the probability from $1/2$ to $2/3$ in favor of switching. Conditioning on partial information requires careful accounting of what the information-revealing event actually tells you.

Randomness is useful in computation for: breaking symmetry, generating cryptographic keys, and designing algorithms that are faster or simpler than known deterministic alternatives. A famous example is **Freivalds' algorithm** for checking matrix multiplication $AB = C$: pick a random vector $r$, check $A(Br) = Cr$. If $AB \neq C$, the check fails with probability $\geq 1/2$. This runs in $O(n^2)$ versus the $O(n^{2.37})$ needed to compute $AB$ directly.

## Probability Puzzles and Conditional Probability

In the Monty Hall problem with 3 doors, switching wins with probability $2/3$. The key: the host's action is *informative* (he knows where the car is). With 100 doors, the host opens 98 goat doors — intuitively, switching wins with probability $99/100$, revealing how much information the host's action carries.

## Randomized Algorithms

**Freivalds' algorithm**: to check if $AB = C$ for $n \times n$ matrices, choose $r \in \{0,1\}^n$ uniformly at random and check $A(Br) \stackrel{?}{=} Cr$. If $AB = C$, this always accepts. If $AB \neq C$, by a Schwartz-Zippel-type argument, the check fails with probability $\geq 1/2$. Running $k$ independent trials reduces the error probability to $\leq 2^{-k}$. This $O(n^2)$-time algorithm is faster than computing $AB$ exactly.
