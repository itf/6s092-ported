# Quantum Algorithms

This lecture covers the most important quantum algorithms, demonstrating that quantum computers can provably outperform classical computers on specific problems. The **Deutsch-Jozsa algorithm** was the first to show a quantum speedup: computing $f(0) \oplus f(1)$ (XOR of a function's two values) requires 2 classical queries but only 1 quantum query. This exponentiates into **Simon's algorithm** and ultimately **Shor's factoring algorithm**.

**Grover's search algorithm** finds a marked item in an unstructured database of $N$ items using $O(\sqrt{N})$ quantum queries, versus $O(N)$ classical queries. This is optimal for quantum computers. **Shor's algorithm** factors an $n$-bit integer in $O(n^3)$ quantum gates by reducing factoring to period-finding, then using the **Quantum Fourier Transform** (QFT) to find the period exponentially faster than any known classical method.

## Deutsch-Jozsa Algorithm

Given oracle access to $f: \{0,1\} \to \{0,1\}$, compute $f(0) \oplus f(1)$ using one quantum query. The circuit: prepare $|+\rangle|-\rangle = H|0\rangle \otimes HX|0\rangle$, apply $U_f: |x,y\rangle \to |x, y \oplus f(x)\rangle$, measure the first qubit after a Hadamard. The key: $U_f$ on the $|-\rangle$ second qubit applies a *phase kickback*, flipping the amplitude of $|x\rangle$ if $f(x) = 1$. After the final Hadamard, you measure 1 iff $f(0) \neq f(1)$.

## Grover's Algorithm and Shor's Algorithm

**Grover's algorithm**: to find a marked item in $N = 2^n$ items, apply the "Grover iteration" $O(\sqrt{N})$ times. Each iteration applies the oracle (flips the phase of the marked item) followed by a "diffusion" step (inversion about the average amplitude). After $\Theta(\sqrt{N})$ iterations, the marked item's amplitude is $\Theta(1)$ and a measurement finds it with high probability. This $\sqrt{N}$ speedup is optimal.

**Shor's algorithm**: to factor $N$, pick random $a < N$ and find the period $r$ of $f(x) = a^x \bmod N$. If $r$ is even and $a^{r/2} \not\equiv -1 \pmod N$, then $\gcd(a^{r/2} \pm 1, N)$ yields non-trivial factors. Period-finding uses the QFT to detect periodicity in $O(n^2)$ quantum gates, giving overall $O(n^3)$ complexity.

<checkyourself>
Why does Grover's algorithm achieve only a *quadratic* speedup (not exponential) over classical search?

<showhide>
Grover's algorithm works by amplitude amplification: it increases the probability of the marked item from $1/N$ to nearly 1 over $O(\sqrt{N})$ iterations. Each iteration rotates the amplitude vector by an angle $\theta \approx 2/\sqrt{N}$, and $O(1/\theta) = O(\sqrt{N})$ rotations are needed to reach the target. A lower bound (BBBV theorem) proves that $\Omega(\sqrt{N})$ quantum queries are necessary for any quantum search algorithm on an unstructured database.
