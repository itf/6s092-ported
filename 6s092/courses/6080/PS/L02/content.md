# Logic

Logic is the foundation of all rigorous reasoning in mathematics and computer science. Aristotle formalized the first logical system — the *syllogism* — which in modern terms is just transitivity of implication: if $A \Rightarrow B$ and $B \Rightarrow C$, then $A \Rightarrow C$. A key insight from the lecture is that our brains were designed to chase small animals, not to prove theorems. We're systematically bad at abstract logical reasoning but good at social reasoning. Translating abstract logic problems into social settings (e.g., a bouncer checking IDs) dramatically improves performance.

Propositional logic extends syllogisms to Boolean variables connected by AND ($\wedge$), OR ($\vee$), and NOT ($\neg$). First-order logic adds quantifiers ($\forall$, $\exists$) and functions, allowing us to express statements like "for all $n$, $\text{Prime}(n) \Rightarrow \text{Prime}(n) \geq 2$." 2SAT — given a propositional formula in conjunctive normal form with at most 2 literals per clause, is it satisfiable? — is solvable in polynomial time using implication graphs.

## Propositional Logic and SAT

A Boolean formula in *conjunctive normal form* (CNF) is an AND of clauses, each clause being an OR of literals. The satisfiability problem (SAT) asks whether there is an assignment of true/false to variables that makes a CNF formula true. 2SAT restricts each clause to at most 2 literals and is efficiently solvable; 3SAT (at most 3 literals per clause) is NP-complete.

## First-Order Logic

First-order logic adds variables, functions, and quantifiers to propositional logic. It can express statements like the infinitude of primes: $\forall n\, \exists p > n,\, \text{Prime}(p)$. First-order logic is *complete* (Gödel's completeness theorem: every valid formula has a proof) but *undecidable* (there is no algorithm that determines whether an arbitrary first-order sentence is valid).
