# Public-key Cryptography

Public-key cryptography allows two parties to communicate securely without ever having met to share a secret key. **Diffie-Hellman key exchange** (1976) was the first such scheme: Alice and Bob each pick random exponents and exchange $g^a \bmod p$ and $g^b \bmod p$ over a public channel. Their shared secret is $g^{ab} \bmod p$. An eavesdropper who sees $g^a$ and $g^b$ must compute $g^{ab}$ from them — believed to require solving the *discrete logarithm problem*, for which no polynomial-time algorithm is known on classical computers.

**RSA** (Rivest-Shamir-Adleman, 1977) is the most widely used public-key cryptosystem. The recipient generates primes $p, q$, sets $N = pq$, and publishes $N$. To encrypt message $x$: compute $x^3 \bmod N$ (or $x^e \bmod N$ for some public exponent $e$). To decrypt: the recipient uses the factorization of $N$ to compute a $k$ with $3k \equiv 1 \pmod{(p-1)(q-1)}$, and recovers $x = (x^3)^k \bmod N$.

## RSA and Number Theory

RSA correctness follows from Euler's theorem: $x^{\phi(N)} \equiv 1 \pmod N$ for $\gcd(x, N) = 1$, where $\phi(N) = (p-1)(q-1)$. If $3k \equiv 1 \pmod{\phi(N)}$, then $x^{3k} = x^{1 + m\phi(N)} = x \cdot (x^{\phi(N)})^m \equiv x \pmod N$. Computing $k$ given $\phi(N)$ uses the extended Euclidean algorithm in polynomial time. The security of RSA relies on the difficulty of factoring $N = pq$.

## One-Way Functions

A **one-way function** is a polytime computable $f: \{0,1\}^n \to \{0,1\}^{p(n)}$ such that no polytime algorithm can invert $f$ on a random input with non-negligible probability. Candidates: multiplication ($f(p,q) = pq$, hard to invert = factoring), discrete exponentiation ($f(x) = g^x \bmod p$), and lattice problems. One-way functions are necessary and sufficient for private-key cryptography (stream ciphers, MACs). For public-key cryptography, we additionally need *trapdoor* one-way functions.
