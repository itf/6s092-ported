# Introduction

Computer science, as Scott Aaronson frames it at the start of 6.080, is not glorified programming. In Dijkstra's famous words, it has about as much to do with computers as astronomy has to do with telescopes. The course presents computer science as a mathematical toolkit for understanding any system — brains, universes, living organisms — by starting from the simplest possible rules and asking what complex behaviors they allow or forbid.

The first lecture introduces several motivating puzzles. One is the *quine*: a program that prints its own source code. A quine in English reads: "Print the following twice, the second time in quotes." This self-referential idea — a program that takes itself as input — will later reappear when we study universal Turing machines, the halting problem, and Gödel's incompleteness theorems.

A second motivating example is online gambling. How can a casino commit to a fair coin flip over the internet without revealing the result early and without cheating? The answer uses a *commitment scheme* based on one-way functions: the casino publishes $H(r \| \text{result})$ before the game, where $H$ is a cryptographic hash and $r$ is a random nonce. After the bet, it reveals $r$ and the result, which anyone can verify. This one example touches computability, complexity, and cryptography — the main themes of the course.

## What Is Computer Science?

Computer science studies what can and cannot be computed, and at what cost. Unlike physics (which looks for laws governing the observed universe), TCS starts from arbitrary formal rules and reasons about what those rules make possible. Euclid's geometry — a handful of axioms from which an entire mathematical edifice is derived — is one of the earliest examples of this style of reasoning. Euclid's GCD algorithm, one of the first non-trivial algorithms in history, shows that these formal systems can also *do* things.

## Infinity and Computation

The lecture also previews Cantor's diagonal argument: there are *more* real numbers than integers, even though both sets are infinite. This distinction between countable and uncountable infinities will later explain why most computational problems cannot possibly have a program that solves them — there are more problems than there are programs.
