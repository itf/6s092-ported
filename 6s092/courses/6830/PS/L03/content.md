# Schema Design

Good schema design avoids redundancy and anomalies. Normalization theory provides a systematic way to decompose relations into well-structured schemas using **functional dependencies** (FDs) and normal forms such as BCNF and 3NF.

## Functional Dependencies

A **functional dependency** $X \\to Y$ holds on relation $R$ if for every two tuples $t_1, t_2$ with $t_1[X] = t_2[X]$, it must be that $t_1[Y] = t_2[Y]$. FDs are constraints on the data, not just observations about a current instance.

<checkyourself>
Given FDs $A \\to B$ and $B \\to C$, can you conclude $A \\to C$? Which rule of Armstrong's axioms justifies this?

<showhide>
Yes. By **transitivity** (one of Armstrong's axioms): if $A \\to B$ and $B \\to C$, then $A \\to C$. Armstrong's axioms (reflexivity, augmentation, transitivity) are sound and complete for deriving all FDs implied by a given set.
</showhide>
</checkyourself>

## Normal Forms

**1NF**: all attribute values are atomic. **2NF**: no partial dependency (no non-key attribute depends on a proper subset of any candidate key). **3NF**: no transitive dependency (no non-key attribute depends on another non-key attribute). **BCNF**: for every FD $X \\to Y$, $X$ is a superkey.
