# Introduction and O/S Review

This lecture introduces MIT 6.824 and motivates the study of distributed systems. The central challenge is building systems that are fast, reliable, and correct despite operating across unreliable networks with independent failures. The course uses a combination of lectures on design techniques and paper-reading case studies, culminating in real systems built in labs.

A key theme is the tension between performance and correctness. A single-server solution (e.g., one mail server) is simple but becomes a bottleneck under load. Adding more servers introduces coordination challenges: how do you keep data consistent across replicas? How do you handle partial failures? Distributed systems engineering is largely about managing these trade-offs.

The lecture reviews operating systems concepts that reappear throughout the course: processes, threads, file descriptors, I/O, and the UNIX system call interface. These abstractions are the building blocks of the distributed services we will construct.

## Why Distributed Systems?

Systems scale beyond what a single machine can handle. Performance, availability, and geographic distribution all motivate running computation and storage across multiple machines. But distribution introduces new failure modes: network partitions, partial crashes, and message reordering. A single-server system can fail atomically; a distributed system can fail in arbitrarily complex ways.

## Performance and Load

A well-designed system should not degrade under high load. A naive server that processes requests serially can become a bottleneck: once it saturates, adding more requests reduces throughput. The goal is a flat throughput curve at the system's maximum capacity—not one that collapses under overload.

Concurrency and load balancing are the primary tools. Multiple servers can share the load, but they must coordinate to avoid inconsistency. The rest of 6.824 explores the design space of these coordination mechanisms.

<checkyourself>
Why does coordination overhead limit the scalability gains from adding more servers?

<showhide>
Servers that share state must communicate to stay consistent. This communication takes time and consumes bandwidth. As more servers are added, the coordination cost grows (sometimes superlinearly), eventually offsetting the gains from additional compute capacity. This is captured by Amdahl's Law: if a fraction $s$ of work is serial (due to coordination), the maximum speedup is $1/s$ regardless of how many servers you add.
