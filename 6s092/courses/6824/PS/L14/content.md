# Viewstamped Replication

Viewstamped Replication (VSR), introduced by Oki and Liskov (1988), is a complete protocol for building a **fault-tolerant replicated service** using a primary-copy approach with Paxos-style view changes. It predates Paxos and addresses the full problem of replication: not just consensus on a single value, but reconstructing state and switching primaries correctly after a failure.

The core insight: replication requires not just agreeing on which operations to apply, but ensuring that when a new primary is elected, it has a complete and correct copy of the service state. VSR uses **quorum-based view changes** to ensure the new primary can always reconstruct the full operation history.

The lecture covers VSR's normal-case protocol, view change procedure, and how it handles the case where a backup misses some operations due to network partition.

## Normal Case Operation

In VSR, there are $2f+1$ replicas ($f+1$ primaries + $f$ backups is the standard terminology). The **primary** processes client requests in order, assigns each a **op-number**, and sends it to all backups. Backups acknowledge; once $f+1$ replicas (including the primary) have accepted an operation, it is **committed**. The primary replies to the client and advances the **commit-number**.

The **view-number** identifies the current primary. All replicas agree on the view-number; if the primary appears to have failed, replicas initiate a **view change** to increment the view-number and elect a new primary.

<checkyourself>
Why does VSR require $f+1$ replicas to acknowledge an operation (not just the primary) before the primary can reply to the client?

<showhide>
If only the primary acknowledged, a client might receive a reply for an operation that is not on any backup. If the primary then crashed, the operation would be lost—the new primary would not know about it. By requiring $f+1$ replicas to acknowledge, at least one replica (that is part of any future majority) is guaranteed to have the operation. The new primary will find it during the view change.
</showhide>
</checkyourself>

## View Change

A view change occurs when backups suspect the primary has failed (timeout). Replicas increment the view-number and run a protocol to elect the new primary (typically the replica with the next-highest ID). The new primary must reconstruct the operation log: it collects the logs from a majority of replicas and takes the union, accepting the highest op-number for each slot.

The view change must ensure that no committed operation is lost. Because a commit required $f+1$ acknowledgments, and the view change contacts a majority ($f+1$ replicas), the intersection guarantees at least one replica in the new view has every committed operation.

How does VSR handle the case where a backup is separated from the primary for a period (misses some operations) and then rejoins the system?

<showhide>
The rejoining backup contacts the current primary (or another replica) and requests the operations it missed—from its last known op-number to the current commit-number. The primary sends the missing log entries, and the backup replays them to bring its state up to date before rejoining the active set. This is called **state transfer**. In Harp (next lecture), a large log is used precisely to make this catch-up efficient, especially for disk-based state where replaying operations is faster than copying the entire disk image.
</showhide>
