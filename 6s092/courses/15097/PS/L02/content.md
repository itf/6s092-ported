## Cross-Validation

**$k$-fold cross-validation** splits data into $k$ equal folds. In each of $k$ iterations, one fold is used as validation and the remaining $k-1$ folds as training. The reported performance is averaged over all folds. This gives a more reliable estimate of out-of-sample performance than a single train/test split.

<checkyourself>
What is the computational trade-off when choosing $k$ in $k$-fold cross-validation? What happens when $k = n$ (leave-one-out cross-validation)?

<showhide>
Larger $k$ means each model is trained on more data (better approximation of full-dataset performance), but requires fitting $k$ models (more computation). When $k = n$ (LOOCV), we fit $n$ models, each on $n-1$ points — this is computationally expensive but gives the least biased estimate. Smaller $k$ (e.g., $k=5$ or $k=10$) is a common practical compromise between bias and computational cost.
</showhide>
</checkyourself>
