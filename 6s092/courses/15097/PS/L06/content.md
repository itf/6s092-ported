# K-Nearest Neighbors

**K-Nearest Neighbors (K-NN)** is one of the simplest machine learning algorithms. It requires no explicit training phase — to classify a new point $x$, find its $K$ nearest training points and predict by majority vote (classification) or weighted average (regression).

## The K-NN Algorithm

**Classification:** Given query point $x$, find the $K$ training points $x_{(1)}, \ldots, x_{(K)}$ nearest to $x$ (by Euclidean distance). Predict $\hat{y} = \text{majority vote}(y_{(1)}, \ldots, y_{(K)})$.

**Regression:** Predict $\hat{y} = \frac{1}{K}\sum_{j=1}^K y_{(j)}$ (or weighted by inverse distance).

K-NN does not explicitly compute a decision boundary. The boundaries between classes form a subset of the **Voronoi diagram** of the training data (for $K=1$).

<checkyourself>
What happens to the decision boundary of K-NN as $K$ increases? What happens as $K \to m$ (total training set size)?

<showhide>
As $K$ increases, the decision boundary becomes smoother (less jagged/complex). For $K=1$, each training point has its own Voronoi cell — the boundary is very complex and follows the training data closely (low bias, high variance). As $K \to m$, all points use all training examples for voting, so the prediction approaches the global majority class everywhere — this is a constant predictor (high bias, zero variance). The optimal $K$ balances bias and variance.
</showhide>

## Distance Metrics

K-NN's performance depends critically on the choice of distance metric. Common metrics:
- **Euclidean (L2):** $d(x, z) = \sqrt{\sum_j (x^{(j)} - z^{(j)})^2}$
- **Manhattan (L1):** $d(x, z) = \sum_j |x^{(j)} - z^{(j)}|$
- **Cosine similarity** (for text)

Feature scaling matters: if features have very different ranges, the distance is dominated by the largest-scale feature. Normalizing features (zero mean, unit variance) is important for K-NN.

## Curse of Dimensionality

In high dimensions, all points become roughly equidistant from any query point, making nearest neighbors meaningless. For $n$ dimensions, the fraction of data within a ball of radius $r$ around a query point shrinks exponentially in $n$. K-NN becomes very data-hungry in high dimensions.

<checkyourself>
Why does K-NN have no explicit "training" phase, and what is the computational cost of making a prediction for a new point?

<showhide>
K-NN is a **lazy learner** — it simply stores all training examples. There is no training phase (no model parameters to optimize). The computational cost is paid at prediction time: to classify a new point, we must compute its distance to all $m$ training points, which takes $O(m \cdot n)$ time (where $n$ is the feature dimension). This makes K-NN expensive at test time for large datasets, unlike "eager" learners (e.g., neural networks, decision trees) that pay the cost upfront at training time.
</showhide>
