# Amortization: Amortized Analysis

**Amortized analysis** gives a tight bound on the average cost of operations in a sequence, even when individual operations vary widely in cost. Unlike average-case analysis, amortized analysis is a worst-case bound on the total cost of $n$ operations—it does not use probability. The three standard methods are the **aggregate method**, the **accounting (banker's) method**, and the **potential (physicist's) method**.

The classic example is a **binary counter**: incrementing a $k$-bit counter can flip up to $k$ bits in one operation, but over $n$ increments, the total number of bit flips is only $O(n)$. Each bit $i$ is flipped at most every $2^i$ increments, so the total flips over $n$ operations is $\sum_{i=0}^{k-1} n/2^i < 2n = O(n)$.

Another key example is **dynamic arrays** (e.g., Python lists). When the array is full, it doubles in size. A single insertion can cost $O(n)$ to copy, but over $n$ insertions, the total cost is $O(n)$, giving an amortized $O(1)$ per insertion.

## The Potential Method

In the **potential method**, we define a **potential function** $\Phi$ mapping data structure states to real numbers, with $\Phi(\text{initial}) = 0$ and $\Phi \geq 0$ always. The **amortized cost** of operation $i$ is $\hat{c}_i = c_i + \Phi_i - \Phi_{i-1}$. The total actual cost is bounded by $\sum \hat{c}_i + \Phi_0 - \Phi_n \leq \sum \hat{c}_i$.

For dynamic arrays, let $\Phi = 2 \cdot (\text{size}) - \text{capacity}$. After a doubling event (inserting into a full array of capacity $m$): $c_i = m + 1$, $\Phi$ drops from $m$ to $2$, so $\hat{c}_i = (m+1) + (2 - m) = 3 = O(1)$.

## Accounting Method

In the **accounting method**, we assign amortized costs $\hat{c}_i$ to operations such that $\hat{c}_i \geq c_i$ always (we can "prepay" for future expensive operations). The excess $\hat{c}_i - c_i$ is stored as **credit** on specific parts of the data structure. We require that credits never go negative.

For the binary counter, assign amortized cost 2 to each increment: 1 unit pays for setting the bit to 1, and 1 unit is stored as credit on the bit. When a bit is reset to 0 (which costs 1), the stored credit pays for it. Each increment stores at most 1 credit, so $n$ increments cost at most $2n$ total.
