# Linear Programming: LP, Reductions, Simplex

**Linear programming (LP)** optimizes a linear objective over a convex polytope defined by linear constraints. The standard form: minimize $c^T x$ subject to $Ax = b$, $x \geq 0$. LP is solvable in polynomial time (ellipsoid method: $O(n^6 L^2)$; interior-point: $O(n^{3.5} L)$) and captures a huge range of optimization problems.

The **Simplex algorithm** is not polynomial in the worst case (exponential on adversarial instances like the Klee-Minty cube) but is very fast in practice. It moves from vertex to vertex of the feasible polytope along improving edges, terminating when no improving edge exists (optimality by convexity).

**LP duality**: every LP has a **dual LP**. The dual of min $c^T x$ s.t. $Ax \geq b$, $x \geq 0$ is max $b^T y$ s.t. $A^T y \leq c$, $y \geq 0$. **Weak duality**: $c^T x \geq b^T y$ for any primal feasible $x$ and dual feasible $y$. **Strong duality** (LP): if the primal has an optimal solution, so does the dual, and their optimal values are equal.

## Geometry of LP

The feasible region of an LP is a **convex polytope** (intersection of halfspaces). The optimal solution is attained at a **vertex** (basic feasible solution) if it exists. The simplex method moves along edges of the polytope; pivoting rules determine which edge to take.

**Simplex pivoting**: at each step, select an entering variable (one that improves the objective) and a leaving variable (ratio test to maintain feasibility). The number of vertices can be exponential, but in practice simplex terminates quickly.

<showhide>
Because the feasible region is a **convex** polytope, any local optimum (no improving neighbor vertex) is a global optimum. If there were a better solution elsewhere, the convex combination of the two optima would be feasible and we could follow a path of improving vertices to reach it. This is in contrast to non-convex problems where local search can get stuck.

## Reductions to LP

Many combinatorial problems reduce to LP: max flow (each edge is a variable, capacity and flow conservation are constraints), shortest paths, bipartite matching (the LP relaxation of bipartite matching is integral—a fundamental fact in combinatorial optimization), and scheduling problems.

**LP relaxation**: replace integer constraints ($x \in \{0,1\}$) with $0 \leq x \leq 1$. The LP relaxation provides an upper bound for maximization problems and can yield fractional solutions. When the constraint matrix is **totally unimodular (TU)**, the LP relaxation always has an integral optimal solution.

What is complementary slackness and how is it used to verify LP optimality?

<showhide>
Complementary slackness conditions: for primal feasible $x$ and dual feasible $y$, they are both optimal iff (1) for each primal variable $x_j > 0$, the corresponding dual constraint is tight (equality), and (2) for each dual variable $y_i > 0$, the corresponding primal constraint is tight. These conditions are necessary and sufficient for joint optimality (given strong duality). They are used in the primal-dual method: construct a dual solution to certify primal optimality, or use violations to guide the primal algorithm.
