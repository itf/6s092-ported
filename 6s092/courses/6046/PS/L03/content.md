# Divide & Conquer: FFT

The **Fast Fourier Transform (FFT)** is one of the most important algorithms in computer science. It computes the **Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT)** of an $n$-element vector in $O(n \log n)$ time, compared to the naive $O(n^2)$. Its applications include polynomial multiplication, signal processing, and integer multiplication.

Given a polynomial $A(x) = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} a_k x^k$ of degree $n-1$, the DFT evaluates $A$ at the $n$-th roots of unity $\omega_n^0, \omega_n^1, \ldots, \omega_n^{n-1}$, where $\omega_n = e^{2\pi i/n}$. The key insight is that these evaluation points have special recursive structure: $(\omega_n^k)^2 = \omega_{n/2}^k$, so $A$ can be split into its even and odd coefficient polynomials $A^{[0]}$ and $A^{[1]}$, each of degree $n/2 - 1$.

The Cooley-Tukey FFT recursively computes the DFT of $A^{[0]}$ and $A^{[1]}$ (each of size $n/2$), then combines: $A(\omega_n^k) = A^{[0]}(\omega_{n/2}^k) + \omega_n^k A^{[1]}(\omega_{n/2}^k)$. This gives $T(n) = 2T(n/2) + O(n) = O(n \log n)$.

## Polynomial Multiplication via FFT

To multiply two degree-$(n-1)$ polynomials $A$ and $B$: (1) evaluate $A$ and $B$ at $2n$ points using FFT in $O(n \log n)$; (2) pointwise multiply in $O(n)$; (3) interpolate (inverse FFT) to get the coefficient representation of $C = A \cdot B$ in $O(n \log n)$. The total cost is $O(n \log n)$, versus $O(n^2)$ for schoolbook multiplication.

The inverse DFT uses the same butterfly structure as the forward DFT, with $\omega_n$ replaced by $\omega_n^{-1} = e^{-2\pi i/n}$, and divides by $n$ at the end. This follows from the identity $\text{DFT}^{-1} = \frac{1}{n} \overline{\text{DFT}}$ (conjugate and scale).

<checkyourself>
Why must we evaluate at $2n$ points (not $n$) when multiplying two degree-$(n-1)$ polynomials?

<showhide>
The product $C = A \cdot B$ has degree $2n-2$, requiring $2n-1$ coefficients. To uniquely determine a degree-$(2n-2)$ polynomial by evaluation, we need at least $2n-1$ points. We round up to $2n$ (or the next power of 2) to use FFT. Evaluating at only $n$ points would cause aliasing: we'd recover the wrong polynomial.
</showhide>
</checkyourself>

## Roots of Unity and the Butterfly Structure

The $n$-th roots of unity are $\omega_n^k = e^{2\pi i k/n}$ for $k = 0, \ldots, n-1$. Key properties: $\omega_n^{n/2} = -1$ (halving lemma), $(\omega_n^k)^2 = \omega_{n/2}^k$ (squaring lemma), and $\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} (\omega_n^j)^k = 0$ for $j \neq 0$ (cancellation lemma). These identities underlie the correctness of the FFT's divide-and-conquer step.

The butterfly network computes the FFT in $\log n$ stages, each doing $n/2$ butterfly operations. Each butterfly combines two values $(a, b)$ to produce $(a + \omega b, a - \omega b)$ for some root of unity $\omega$.

What is the inverse FFT, and how does it relate to the forward FFT?

<showhide>
The inverse DFT can be computed using the same FFT algorithm but replacing $\omega_n$ with $\omega_n^{-1} = e^{-2\pi i/n}$ and dividing the result by $n$. This follows from the fact that the DFT matrix $F$ satisfies $F^{-1} = \frac{1}{n}\bar{F}$ (conjugate and scale). Thus the inverse FFT has the same $O(n \log n)$ complexity as the forward FFT.
