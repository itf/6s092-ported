# BGP Routing

The internet is composed of thousands of independently administered networks called **Autonomous Systems (ASes)**. Routing within an AS uses interior gateway protocols (IGPs) like OSPF or RIP. Routing between ASes uses **BGP (Border Gateway Protocol)**—the glue that holds the internet together.

BGP is a **path-vector** protocol: routers advertise not just distances but complete AS paths to prefixes. Path vectors prevent routing loops (a router rejects any advertisement containing its own AS number) and allow **policy-based routing**—each AS can choose which routes to accept and advertise based on business relationships (customer, provider, peer) rather than just shortest paths.

BGP scalability is achieved through **aggregation**: instead of advertising millions of individual /32 host routes, ASes advertise aggregated prefixes (e.g., 18.0.0.0/8 covers all MIT addresses). The routing table at a major router contains ~900,000 prefixes rather than billions of host routes. **Longest prefix match** resolves conflicts when multiple prefixes cover a destination.

## BGP Policy and Relationships

ASes have business relationships: a **customer** pays a **provider** for transit; **peers** exchange traffic for free (settlement-free peering). Route selection follows economic logic: prefer customer routes (revenue) over peer routes (free) over provider routes (cost). This creates a routing hierarchy that mirrors the internet's economic structure.

Why does BGP use path vectors rather than distance vectors, and what routing problem does this solve?

<showhide>
Path vectors include the full sequence of ASes on the path. When a router receives an advertisement, it checks whether its own AS is already in the path. If so, accepting the route would create a loop, and the advertisement is discarded. Distance vectors only carry a metric, not the path, so a router can unknowingly advertise routes that form loops (the count-to-infinity problem). Path vectors completely prevent loops at the AS level.

## Prefix Aggregation and Longest Prefix Match

Aggregation reduces routing table size. A provider that allocates 203.0.113.0/25 and 203.0.113.128/25 to two customers can advertise just 203.0.113.0/24 to the rest of the internet, reducing two advertisements to one. Longest prefix match ensures that more-specific prefixes override the aggregate when needed.

What is a BGP route leak and why is it harmful?

<showhide>
A route leak occurs when an AS re-advertises a route it received from one neighbor to another neighbor in a way that violates BGP policy. For example, if AS2 receives a route from peer AS3 and re-advertises it to provider AS4, AS4 might route traffic through AS2 to AS3—making AS2 a transit provider between AS4 and AS3 without compensation. This can cause traffic to take unexpected paths, enabling traffic interception, and can overload AS2's links. Notable real-world leaks (e.g., Pakistan Telecom in 2008) have caused major internet outages.
