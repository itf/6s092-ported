# Bounded Buffers and Locks

Enforced modularity breaks down when modules share resources. When two threads (or processes) access a shared variable without coordination, the result depends on the interleaving of their operations—a **race condition**. Race conditions are notoriously hard to debug because they only manifest under specific timing conditions.

**Locks** (also called mutexes) provide **mutual exclusion**: at most one thread holds the lock at a time. A thread that wants to access a shared resource acquires the lock, performs the access, and releases the lock. Any other thread that tries to acquire the lock while it is held will **block** until it is released. Locks are the simplest synchronization primitive.

**Bounded buffers** (producer-consumer queues) are a fundamental shared data structure: producers insert items, consumers remove them, and the buffer has a fixed capacity. A correct implementation requires coordination to avoid: (1) a producer inserting into a full buffer, (2) a consumer removing from an empty buffer, and (3) concurrent insertions/removals corrupting the buffer state.

## Race Conditions

A race condition occurs when the program's outcome depends on the relative timing of thread operations. Even a seemingly simple operation like `x = x + 1` is not atomic: it reads x, increments, and writes back. If two threads do this concurrently, one increment may be lost.

## Lock Correctness

A lock must satisfy two properties: **mutual exclusion** (at most one thread holds the lock at a time) and **progress** (if no thread holds the lock and threads are waiting, one eventually acquires it). Deadlock occurs when threads wait for each other in a cycle: A holds lock 1 and waits for lock 2; B holds lock 2 and waits for lock 1.

<checkyourself>
Why is it insufficient to protect a bounded buffer with a single lock that covers all operations? What additional mechanism is needed?

<showhide>
A single lock ensures mutual exclusion (no two threads modify the buffer simultaneously), but it doesn't help a consumer that finds the buffer empty—the consumer holds the lock and cannot make progress but also cannot release it safely without a signaling mechanism. **Condition variables** (or semaphores) are needed: a consumer that finds the buffer empty releases the lock and waits on a "not empty" condition; a producer that inserts an item signals that condition to wake the waiting consumer.
