# Performance

Performance analysis starts with identifying **bottlenecks**: the resource whose saturation limits overall throughput. Little's Law relates throughput, latency, and concurrency. Systematic performance improvement follows a principled approach: measure, find the bottleneck, address it, repeat.

General techniques for improving performance include **caching** (store the result of expensive operations for reuse), **batching** (amortize fixed costs over multiple operations), **concurrency** (overlap independent operations), and **parallelism** (use multiple resources simultaneously). Each technique has a domain where it applies and tradeoffs in complexity, consistency, and resource usage.

**Latency vs. throughput** is a fundamental tension. Optimizing for low latency often means processing requests immediately (little batching, low concurrency per request). Optimizing for high throughput often means accumulating work and processing it in bulk. Real systems must balance both based on workload characteristics.

## Bottleneck Analysis

A system's throughput is bounded by its slowest (most-utilized) resource. Adding capacity to a non-bottleneck yields no improvement. **Amdahl's Law** quantifies the limit of parallelization: if a fraction $s$ of work is serial, the maximum speedup is $1/s$.

A web server spends 80% of its time in database queries and 20% in computation. You optimize the computation to be instantaneous. By what factor does overall latency improve?

<showhide>
The computation was 20% of total time. Eliminating it gives a speedup of $1 / (1 - 0.20) = 1 / 0.80 = 1.25$×—only 25% improvement. The database queries (80% of time) are the bottleneck and are untouched. This illustrates why you must optimize the bottleneck, not the easy parts.

## Caching and Batching

**Caching** exploits **temporal locality**: recently used data is likely to be used again. Cache effectiveness is measured by the **hit rate**. A cache with hit rate $h$ and cache hit latency $t_c$ and miss latency $t_m$ has average access time $h \cdot t_c + (1-h) \cdot t_m$.

**Batching** amortizes fixed per-operation costs. If an operation has fixed cost $F$ and per-item cost $V$, processing a batch of $B$ items costs $F + B \cdot V$ rather than $B \cdot (F + V)$—saving $(B-1) \cdot F$.

When does caching hurt rather than help performance?

<showhide>
Caching hurts when: (1) the hit rate is very low (cache thrashing—data is loaded and evicted before reuse), (2) cache maintenance overhead exceeds the savings (e.g., very small, cheap datasets), (3) stale cache data causes correctness problems that require expensive invalidation, or (4) the cache is too small for the working set, causing frequent evictions and reloads (thrashing). In these cases, the overhead of maintaining the cache and the latency of cache misses outweigh the benefits.
