# Distributed Transactions

Single-machine transactions are implemented with local locks and a local log. Distributed transactions span multiple machines—each holding a shard of the data. Achieving atomicity across machines requires coordination: all participants must either commit or abort together.

**Two-phase commit (2PC)** is the standard protocol for distributed atomicity. A **coordinator** manages the protocol; each **participant** holds a shard. Phase 1 (prepare): the coordinator sends PREPARE to all participants; each participant decides whether it can commit (logs "prepared" durably) and votes YES or NO. Phase 2 (commit/abort): if all vote YES, the coordinator sends COMMIT to all and logs COMMIT; if any votes NO, it sends ABORT.

2PC is a **blocking protocol**: if the coordinator crashes after phase 1 but before phase 2, participants that voted YES are stuck—they hold locks but cannot commit or abort without hearing from the coordinator. This is the fundamental limitation of 2PC.

## Two-Phase Commit in Detail

The durability guarantee: a participant that votes YES has logged its "prepared" state durably. Even if it crashes and restarts, it knows it voted YES and will wait for the coordinator's decision. The coordinator's COMMIT/ABORT log record is the single point of truth; once the coordinator logs COMMIT, the outcome is determined.

Why is 2PC called a "blocking" protocol, and what happens when the coordinator fails after participants have voted YES?

<showhide>
2PC is blocking because a participant that has voted YES has committed to either committing or aborting—it cannot decide unilaterally. If the coordinator crashes after receiving YES votes (but before sending COMMIT/ABORT), participants hold locks and wait. They cannot commit (the coordinator might decide ABORT) and cannot abort (the coordinator might decide COMMIT). The system is blocked until the coordinator recovers. This is the fundamental blocking nature of 2PC, and it's why availability suffers during coordinator failures.

## Coordinator Failure and Recovery

The coordinator's commit record is the commit point. If the coordinator crashes: (a) before logging COMMIT, it aborts the transaction on recovery (participants may have voted YES but coordinator will send ABORT). (b) after logging COMMIT, it resends COMMIT to all participants on recovery (they may need to re-apply the commit).

What is three-phase commit (3PC) and why does it avoid the blocking problem of 2PC, and what assumption does it require?

<showhide>
3PC adds a "pre-commit" phase between PREPARE and COMMIT, and introduces timeouts that allow participants to make a decision even without the coordinator. The key: if a participant times out waiting for the coordinator and it's in the pre-commit phase, it can safely commit (all participants must have voted YES to reach pre-commit). This avoids the blocked-in-prepared state. However, 3PC requires a **no-partition** assumption: it can make incorrect decisions if network partitions cause some participants to think the coordinator failed while the coordinator is still running. 3PC works under crash-stop failures but not under network partitions, so it is rarely used in practice.
</showhide>
