# In-Network Resource Management

Routers have finite buffers. When a burst of traffic arrives faster than a link can drain it, packets queue in the router's buffer. If the buffer fills, packets are dropped. The discipline governing how packets are queued and when they are dropped is **in-network resource management**.

**Queue management** schemes include tail-drop (drop new arrivals when the queue is full—simple but causes TCP synchronization), **RED (Random Early Detection)** (probabilistically drop packets before the queue is full to signal congestion early), and **AQM (Active Queue Management)** algorithms like CoDel (target a queue delay rather than a queue length).

**Traffic differentiation** allows routers to give different service levels to different traffic classes. **Weighted Fair Queuing (WFQ)** maintains separate queues per flow and services them in proportion to their weights. **Priority queuing** always serves higher-priority traffic first. These mechanisms are used for **Quality of Service (QoS)** guarantees—e.g., ensuring VoIP packets have low latency.

## Queue Management and Bufferbloat

A large buffer allows bursts without dropping but introduces high queueing delay—**bufferbloat**. With bufferbloat, the buffer is always nearly full; end-to-end latency is dominated by queueing delay rather than propagation delay. AQM addresses this by maintaining a short target queue, signaling congestion early enough that TCP backs off before the buffer fills.

<checkyourself>
What is the bufferbloat problem, and why does RED/AQM help solve it?

<showhide>
Bufferbloat: large buffers cause high, variable queueing latency. TCP fills the buffer to capacity and only backs off when packets drop. With a 100 ms buffer at a 10 Mbps link, the buffer holds ~125 KB, causing up to 100 ms of extra latency even when the link is only lightly congested. AQM (e.g., CoDel) signals congestion by dropping/marking packets when the queue delay exceeds a target (e.g., 5 ms), forcing TCP to reduce its rate before the buffer fills. This keeps queue latency low while still allowing bursts.

## Traffic Differentiation and Scheduling

WFQ assigns each flow a share of link bandwidth proportional to its weight. A flow with weight 2 gets twice the bandwidth of a flow with weight 1, even when competing. WFQ provides fairness and prevents a high-bandwidth flow from starving low-bandwidth flows.

Why is priority queuing potentially dangerous in a multi-tenant network, and how does WFQ address this?

<showhide>
Priority queuing can **starve** low-priority flows: if high-priority traffic is always present, low-priority packets never get served. A malicious or misbehaving application could send all traffic at high priority, monopolizing the link. WFQ avoids starvation by guaranteeing each flow a minimum share proportional to its weight. A high-weight flow cannot completely starve a low-weight flow—the low-weight flow always receives its proportional share.
