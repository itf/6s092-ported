# The Course at a Glance

The central challenge of machine learning is to approximate an unknown multivariate function from sparse, noisy data. Regularization theory provides a principled framework: rather than simply minimizing empirical error, we penalize complexity to prevent overfitting. The course unifies classical approximation theory, functional analysis, and statistical learning theory around this theme.

Two cornerstones drive the course. First, **Tikhonov regularization** in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) gives a constructive solution that includes Support Vector Machines as a special case. Second, **Learning Theory** provides generalization bounds—quantitative assurances that the function found on training data performs well on unseen examples. The interplay between these perspectives is what makes 9.520 distinctive.

Applications span object recognition in computer vision, financial time-series forecasting, and neuroscience models of the visual cortex. A recurring theme is that the same mathematical machinery (regularization in RKHS) underlies seemingly different algorithms, from splines to SVMs to manifold-regularization methods.

## Learning from Examples as Function Approximation

Given training data $\{(x_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^n$ drawn i.i.d. from an unknown distribution $\rho$ on $\mathcal{X} \times \mathcal{Y}$, the goal is to find $f: \mathcal{X} \to \mathcal{Y}$ that minimizes the **true risk** $I[f] = \int \ell(f(x), y)\, d\rho$, where $\ell$ is a loss function.

<checkyourself>
Why can't we directly minimize the true risk $I[f]$, and what do we minimize instead?

<showhide>
We cannot minimize $I[f]$ because the distribution $\rho$ is unknown. Instead we minimize the **empirical risk** $I_S[f] = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \ell(f(x_i), y_i)$ computed on the available training data $S = \{(x_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^n$.

## Tikhonov Regularization Overview

The Tikhonov regularization problem takes the form:

$$f^* = \arg\min_{f \in \mathcal{H}} \left[ \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (f(x_i) - y_i)^2 + \lambda \|f\|_{\mathcal{H}}^2 \right]$$

where $\lambda > 0$ is the **regularization parameter** and $\|\cdot\|_{\mathcal{H}}$ is the norm in the hypothesis space $\mathcal{H}$.
