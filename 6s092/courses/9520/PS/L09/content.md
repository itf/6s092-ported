# Ranking

**Ranking** (or learning to rank) is the task of ordering items by relevance, rather than assigning absolute labels. Given a set of items $\{x_1,\ldots,x_n\}$ and a scoring function $f: \mathcal{X} \to \mathbb{R}$, the predicted ranking is the ordering induced by $f(x_i)$. Applications include web search (rank documents by relevance to a query), recommender systems, and bioinformatics.

The **pairwise approach** reduces ranking to binary classification: for each pair $(i,j)$ where item $i$ should rank above item $j$, train $f$ to satisfy $f(x_i) > f(x_j)$. The **RankSVM** minimizes $\frac{1}{2}\|w\|^2 + C\sum_{(i,j) \in \mathcal{P}} \xi_{ij}$ subject to $w\cdot(x_i - x_j) \geq 1 - \xi_{ij}$, where $\mathcal{P}$ is the set of preference pairs. This is equivalent to SVM on difference vectors $x_i - x_j$.

The **Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG)** is a common evaluation metric: $\text{NDCG}@k = \frac{1}{Z_k}\sum_{i=1}^k \frac{2^{r_i}-1}{\log_2(i+1)}$, where $r_i$ is the relevance of the item at position $i$ and $Z_k$ normalizes by the ideal ranking. NDCG is non-differentiable, motivating surrogate losses.

<checkyourself>
Why can ranking not be reduced to standard multi-class classification? What structure of the output space makes ranking different?

<showhide>
In multiclass classification, labels are unordered categories. Ranking requires a total order over items, and the "correct" output depends on the full set of items being ranked, not just the individual labels. The number of possible rankings grows as $n!$, and the relevant structure is the pairwise preference relation $\succ$ rather than class membership.

<checkyourself>
Why is it difficult to directly optimize NDCG in a gradient-based learning algorithm?

<showhide>
NDCG depends on the rank positions of items, which are determined by the ordering induced by the scoring function $f$. Small changes in $f(x_i)$ that don't change the ordering don't change NDCG at all, while a small change that swaps two items changes NDCG discontinuously. Thus NDCG is piecewise constant and has zero gradient almost everywhere — gradient-based optimization cannot proceed. Surrogate losses (hinge, cross-entropy) are used instead.
</showhide>
</checkyourself>
