# The Learning Problem in Perspective

The learning problem begins with data $S = \{(x_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^n$ drawn i.i.d. from an unknown distribution $\rho$ on $\mathcal{X} \times \mathcal{Y}$. We seek a hypothesis $f: \mathcal{X} \to \mathcal{Y}$ with small **true risk** $I[f] = \mathbb{E}_{(x,y)\sim\rho}[\ell(f(x), y)]$. Since $\rho$ is unknown, we minimize the **empirical risk** $I_S[f] = \frac{1}{n}\sum_i \ell(f(x_i), y_i)$ over a hypothesis space $\mathcal{H}$.

The generalization error decomposes as: $I[f_\mathcal{H}] - I[f_\rho] = \underbrace{(I[f_\mathcal{H}] - \inf_{f\in\mathcal{H}} I[f])}_{\text{sample error}} + \underbrace{(\inf_{f\in\mathcal{H}} I[f] - I[f_\rho])}_{\text{approximation error}}$. A small $\mathcal{H}$ reduces sample error but increases approximation error; regularization manages this tradeoff by implicitly shrinking the effective hypothesis space.

Two key properties of good learning algorithms are **stability** (small perturbation of the training set leads to small change in the output) and **consistency** (the solution converges to the best possible function as $n \to \infty$). Tikhonov regularization achieves both.

## Empirical Risk and True Risk

<checkyourself>
Why does minimizing empirical risk alone (without regularization) often lead to overfitting?

<showhide>
Without constraints, empirical risk minimization over a rich hypothesis class can achieve zero training error by memorizing the training data (e.g., interpolating all points). Such an $f$ may have large true risk because it captures noise rather than the underlying pattern. Regularization penalizes complexity to prevent this.

## Stability and Consistency

A learning algorithm $A$ is **uniformly stable** with parameter $\beta$ if replacing any single training example changes the output function by at most $\beta$ in sup-norm: $\sup_x |A_S(x) - A_{S^i}(x)| \leq \beta$, where $S^i$ is $S$ with the $i$-th example replaced.

<checkyourself>
Explain in one sentence the difference between consistency and stability for a learning algorithm.

<showhide>
**Stability** measures sensitivity to small perturbations of the training data (algorithmic property), while **consistency** means the output converges to the best possible function as $n \to \infty$ (asymptotic performance property). Stability implies consistency under mild conditions.
