# Support Vector Machines for Classification

The **Support Vector Machine (SVM)** for binary classification finds the maximum-margin hyperplane separating two classes. Geometrically, given labeled data $(x_i, y_i) \in \mathbb{R}^d \times \{-1,+1\}$, the hard-margin SVM solves $\min_{w,b} \frac{1}{2}\|w\|^2$ subject to $y_i(w \cdot x_i + b) \geq 1$ for all $i$. The **margin** $2/\|w\|$ is maximized by minimizing $\|w\|^2$.

Via Lagrangian duality, the SVM dual is $\max_\alpha \sum_i \alpha_i - \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i,j} \alpha_i \alpha_j y_i y_j x_i \cdot x_j$ subject to $\alpha_i \geq 0$ and $\sum_i \alpha_i y_i = 0$. Replacing $x_i \cdot x_j$ with a kernel $K(x_i, x_j)$ extends SVMs to nonlinear decision boundaries. The soft-margin SVM adds slack variables $\xi_i \geq 0$ and a penalty $C\sum_i \xi_i$ to handle non-separable data.

From the **regularization perspective**, the soft-margin SVM with hinge loss is equivalent to Tikhonov regularization with $\ell(f,y) = \max(0, 1-yf)$ and $\lambda = 1/(2nC)$. This unifies SVMs with the broader regularization framework of the course.

## Maximum Margin and Duality

<checkyourself>
Why do support vectors (training points with $\alpha_i > 0$) lie exactly on the margin boundaries? Use the KKT complementary slackness conditions.

<showhide>
KKT complementary slackness says $\alpha_i(y_i(w \cdot x_i + b) - 1) = 0$. If $\alpha_i > 0$, then $y_i(w \cdot x_i + b) = 1$, meaning the point lies exactly on the margin boundary $\pm 1$. Points with $y_i(w \cdot x_i + b) > 1$ are interior and have $\alpha_i = 0$ — they do not affect the decision boundary.
</showhide>

<checkyourself>
Explain why the kernel trick allows SVMs to learn nonlinear decision boundaries without explicitly computing high-dimensional feature maps.

<showhide>
The SVM dual and prediction both depend on the data only through inner products $x_i \cdot x_j$. Replacing these with $K(x_i, x_j) = \phi(x_i) \cdot \phi(x_j)$ implicitly maps data to a (potentially infinite-dimensional) feature space. Since we never need $\phi(x)$ explicitly — only $K(x_i, x_j)$ — we avoid the cost of computing high-dimensional features. The Gaussian kernel, for example, corresponds to an infinite-dimensional feature space.
</showhide>
</checkyourself>
