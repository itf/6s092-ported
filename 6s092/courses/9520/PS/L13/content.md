# Stability of Tikhonov Regularization

We now prove that Tikhonov regularization in RKHS is **uniformly stable** with $\beta = \kappa^2/(n\lambda)$, where $\kappa = \sup_x \sqrt{K(x,x)}$ bounds the kernel values. This combined with the stability-based generalization bound from the previous lecture gives a complete generalization guarantee for RLS, RLSC, and SVMs.

The proof uses two key tools: (1) **strong convexity** of the regularized objective (the $\lambda\|f\|^2$ term makes the objective strongly convex, so the minimizer is unique and responds smoothly to data perturbations), and (2) **RKHS boundedness** (the reproducing property gives $|f(x)| \leq \|f\|_{\mathcal{H}_K}\sqrt{K(x,x)} \leq \|f\|_{\mathcal{H}_K}\kappa$, bounding pointwise function values by the RKHS norm).

The stability bound $\beta = \kappa^2/(n\lambda)$ shows that with the choice $\lambda = \kappa^2/(\epsilon n)$, we get $\beta = \epsilon/n$. Combined with the Bousquet-Elisseeff bound, Tikhonov regularization achieves $O(1/\sqrt{n})$ generalization error with high probability.

## Strong Convexity and Stability

<checkyourself>
Why does strong convexity of the objective imply stability? Give an intuitive argument.

<showhide>
A strongly convex function has a unique minimum that is "stable" in the sense that the minimum moves only a bounded distance when the function changes slightly. If replacing one training point changes the empirical risk by at most $\Delta$, strong convexity (with parameter $\lambda$) ensures the minimizer changes by at most $O(\Delta/\lambda)$. Larger $\lambda$ gives stronger convexity and hence better stability.

<showhide>
