# Neuroscience and Learning

The visual cortex performs remarkable feats of object recognition, motivating computational models that bridge neuroscience and machine learning. The **ventral stream** (V1 → V2 → V4 → IT cortex) processes visual information hierarchically, with neurons in later areas representing increasingly complex and invariant features.

The **HMAX model** (Riesenhuber & Poggio, 1999) mimics the ventral stream with two cell types: **S-cells** (simple cells) compute local template matches (Gaussian-weighted dot products), and **C-cells** (complex cells) pool over spatial positions and scales via max-pooling. Alternating S and C layers build hierarchical representations invariant to position and scale.

Thomas Serre's 2006 thesis extends HMAX to a full **dictionary of shape components** learned from natural images, demonstrating that such a model accounts for key properties of V1 simple cells (Gabor-like filters) and achieves human-level performance on rapid categorization tasks. This exemplifies how learning algorithms inform neuroscience and vice versa.

## Hierarchical Feature Learning

<checkyourself>
Explain the difference between S-cells (simple cells) and C-cells (complex cells) in the HMAX model. What is each cell's computation?

<showhide>
**S-cells** compute a similarity between the local image patch and a stored template using a Gaussian-weighted dot product (or radial basis function). They respond to specific oriented features at specific positions and scales. **C-cells** pool over a neighborhood of S-cells via max-pooling, creating invariance to small shifts in position and scale. This mimics the physiological distinction between simple cells (orientation-selective, position-specific) and complex cells (orientation-selective but position-tolerant) in V1.
