# Lecture 1: Introduction and Voting Classifiers

This lecture introduces the statistical learning theory framework. We consider the binary classification problem where $Y = \{-1, +1\}$, and study families of classifiers $H = \{h : X \to \{-1, +1\}\}$. The empirical risk minimizer chooses $h_0 = \arg\min_{h \in H} \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \mathbf{1}[h(X_i) \neq Y_i]$. A central question is: when does small training error imply small generalization error?

## Classification Setup

In the classification setting we observe $n$ i.i.d. samples $(X_1, Y_1), \ldots, (X_n, Y_n)$ drawn from some unknown distribution $P$. A classifier $h$ maps inputs to labels. The *training error* is the fraction of misclassified training examples, while the *generalization error* (or *test error*) is $\Pr(h(X) \neq Y)$ under the true distribution.

<checkyourself>
Why might minimizing training error not be sufficient to guarantee good generalization performance?

<showhide>
A classifier can overfit to the training data — memorizing labels without capturing the underlying pattern. With enough expressive power, a classifier can achieve zero training error on any dataset while having near-random generalization. The gap between training and generalization error depends on the complexity of the hypothesis class $H$.
</showhide>
</checkyourself>

## Voting Classifiers

A *voting classifier* combines $T$ weak classifiers $h_1, \ldots, h_T \in H$ with weights $\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_T \geq 0$, $\sum_t \alpha_t = 1$:
$$f(x) = \text{sign}\left(\sum_{t=1}^T \alpha_t h_t(x)\right).$$
The goal is to produce a strong classifier from many weak ones, each of which does only slightly better than random guessing.
