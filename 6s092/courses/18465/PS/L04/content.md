# Lecture 4: Generalization Error of SVM

This lecture derives a bound on the generalization error of SVMs using the *leave-one-out* (LOO) technique. The key insight is that the average generalization error equals the expected leave-one-out error, and the LOO error of SVMs can be bounded in terms of the number of support vectors and the ratio $D^2/m^2$.

## Leave-One-Out Error

For i.i.d. samples $z_1, \ldots, z_n$, the Average Generalization Error (A.G.E.) equals the expected leave-one-out error:
$$\text{A.G.E.} = E\left[\frac{1}{n+1}\sum_{i=1}^{n+1} \mathbf{1}[f_{z_1,\ldots,\hat{z}_i,\ldots,z_{n+1}}(x_i) \neq y_i]\right].$$

This symmetry argument (exchangeability of i.i.d. samples) is fundamental to the LOO analysis.

<checkyourself>
Explain why the average generalization error equals the expected leave-one-out error for i.i.d. data.

<showhide>
Since $z_1, \ldots, z_{n+1}$ are i.i.d., the distribution is symmetric: training on any $n$ of them and testing on the remaining one gives the same expectation. In expectation, training on $z_1,\ldots,z_n$ and testing on $z_{n+1}$ is equivalent to training on $z_1,\ldots,z_{i-1},z_{i+1},\ldots,z_{n+1}$ and testing on $z_i$. Averaging over all $i$ gives the LOO error.
</showhide>
</checkyourself>

## LOO Bound for SVM

**Theorem:** Let $D$ be the diameter of the ball containing all data points and $m$ the margin of the optimal SVM hyperplane. Then:
$$\text{L.O.O.E.} \leq \frac{\min(\text{\# support vectors},\; D^2/m^2)}{n+1}.$$

**Key lemma:** If $x_i$ is a support vector that gets misclassified when left out, then $\alpha_i^0 \geq 1/D^2$.
