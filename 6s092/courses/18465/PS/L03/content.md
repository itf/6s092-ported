# Lecture 3: Support Vector Machines (SVM)

Support Vector Machines find a maximum-margin hyperplane to separate binary-labeled data. Given $X = \mathbb{R}^d$, $Y = \{+1, -1\}$, a hyperplane is parameterized by $\psi \in \mathbb{R}^d$ (with $|\psi|=1$) and bias $b$. The SVM optimization maximizes the margin — the minimum distance from any training point to the hyperplane.

## Maximum Margin Formulation

The signed distance from point $x_i$ to hyperplane $(\psi, b)$ is $y_i(\psi \cdot x_i + b)$. SVM maximizes the margin:
$$\max_{\psi, b} \min_i y_i(\psi \cdot x_i + b).$$

After rescaling so the margin equals 1, this is equivalent to:
$$\min_\psi |\psi|^2 / 2 \quad \text{subject to } y_i(\psi \cdot x_i + b) \geq 1 \text{ for all } i.$$

<showhide>
A large margin means the classifier is robust to small perturbations of the data points. Points near the decision boundary are the hardest to classify; by pushing them away, we reduce sensitivity to noise. Formally, the generalization bound for SVMs depends on $D^2/m^2$ (data diameter over margin squared), so a larger margin $m$ gives a tighter bound.

## Dual Formulation

Introducing Lagrange multipliers $\alpha_i \geq 0$, the dual problem is:
$$\max_{\alpha \geq 0} \sum_i \alpha_i - \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i,j} \alpha_i \alpha_j y_i y_j x_i \cdot x_j \quad \text{subject to } \sum_i \alpha_i y_i = 0.$$
The solution satisfies $\psi = \sum_i \alpha_i y_i x_i$. Training points with $\alpha_i > 0$ are called *support vectors*.
