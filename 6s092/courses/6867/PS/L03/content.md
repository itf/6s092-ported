# Maximum Margin Classification

Among all linear classifiers that correctly separate the training data, the **maximum margin classifier** (hard-margin SVM) chooses the one that maximizes the geometric margin — the distance from the decision boundary to the nearest training point. This leads to a convex quadratic optimization problem.

## Geometric Margin

For a hyperplane defined by $(w, b)$ with $\|w\| = 1$, the geometric margin of a point $(x^{(i)}, y^{(i)})$ is:
$$\gamma^{(i)} = y^{(i)} (w \cdot x^{(i)} + b)$$

The margin of the classifier is $\gamma = \min_i \gamma^{(i)}$.

<checkyourself>
What does it mean geometrically when the margin is large?

<showhide>
A large margin means the nearest training points are far from the decision boundary. The classifier is more confident in its predictions and is generally more robust to perturbations.
</showhide>
</checkyourself>

## Hard-Margin SVM Optimization

Maximizing the margin is equivalent to minimizing $\|w\|^2$ subject to the constraints $y^{(i)}(w \cdot x^{(i)} + b) \geq 1$ for all $i$:
$$\min_{w,b} \frac{1}{2} \|w\|^2 \quad \text{s.t.} \quad y^{(i)}(w \cdot x^{(i)} + b) \geq 1, \forall i$$

<checkyourself>
When does the hard-margin SVM fail to have a solution?

<showhide>
The hard-margin SVM requires the data to be linearly separable. If the classes overlap (no hyperplane correctly separates all training points), the constraint set is empty and the problem is infeasible. In that case, the soft-margin SVM (which allows violations) must be used.
