# Support Vector Machines, Kernels, and Kernel Optimization

The **kernelized SVM** combines the maximum-margin idea with the kernel trick, enabling non-linear decision boundaries. The SVM dual formulation expresses the problem in terms of kernel evaluations, and the optimal classifier is a sparse linear combination (in feature space) of support vectors. Solving SVMs involves quadratic programming, and the **KKT conditions** characterize the solution.

## Dual SVM Formulation

The dual of the hard-margin SVM is:
$$\max_\alpha \sum_i \alpha_i - \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i,j} \alpha_i \alpha_j y^{(i)} y^{(j)} K(x^{(i)}, x^{(j)})$$
subject to $\alpha_i \geq 0$ and $\sum_i \alpha_i y^{(i)} = 0$.

The decision function is: $f(x) = \text{sign}\!\left(\sum_i \alpha_i y^{(i)} K(x^{(i)}, x) + b\right)$.

<checkyourself>
In the dual SVM, which training examples have $\alpha_i > 0$?

<showhide>
Only the **support vectors** — the training examples lying on the margin boundary — have $\alpha_i > 0$. All other examples have $\alpha_i = 0$ and do not contribute to the decision function.
</showhide>
</checkyourself>

## KKT Conditions

At the optimum, complementary slackness requires $\alpha_i (y^{(i)} f(x^{(i)}) - 1) = 0$. This means either $\alpha_i = 0$ (not a support vector) or $y^{(i)} f(x^{(i)}) = 1$ (on the margin).

<checkyourself>
Why does using a kernel with the SVM not change the fact that the SVM solves a convex QP?

<showhide>
Substituting the kernel $K(x^{(i)}, x^{(j)})$ into the dual objective does not break convexity: the kernel matrix $K$ is positive semidefinite (for a valid kernel), so the dual objective is still a concave quadratic in $\alpha$, and the constraints remain linear. The problem is therefore still a convex QP.
</showhide>
