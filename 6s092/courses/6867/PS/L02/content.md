# Perceptron Convergence and Generalization

The perceptron convergence theorem gives a bound on the number of mistakes made before the perceptron converges, in terms of the **margin** of the data. Beyond training accuracy, we care about **generalization**: how well the learned classifier performs on new, unseen data. Key quantities are the number of mistakes $M$, the margin $\gamma$, and the radius $R$ of the data.

## Perceptron Convergence Theorem

If the training data is linearly separable with margin $\gamma$ (there exists a unit-norm $w^*$ such that $y^{(i)} w^* \cdot x^{(i)} \geq \gamma$ for all $i$), and all examples satisfy $\|x^{(i)}\| \leq R$, then the number of mistakes made by the perceptron is bounded by:
$$M \leq \frac{R^2}{\gamma^2}$$

<checkyourself>
What happens to the mistake bound if the margin $\gamma$ is halved?

<showhide>
The bound $M \leq R^2/\gamma^2$ scales as $1/\gamma^2$. If $\gamma$ is halved, the bound quadruples — the perceptron may make up to 4 times as many mistakes.
</showhide>
</checkyourself>

## Generalization

A classifier that fits training data perfectly may still fail on new examples — this is **overfitting**. For a linear classifier, a larger margin on training data generally implies better generalization. The **test error** measures performance on held-out data.

Why does a larger margin imply better generalization for linear classifiers?

<showhide>
A larger margin means the classifier is more confident and robust: small perturbations to the input or to the weight vector do not change the prediction. Formally, margin-based generalization bounds show test error decreases as training margin increases.
