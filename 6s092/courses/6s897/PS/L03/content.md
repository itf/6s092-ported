# Deep Dive into Clinical Data

This lecture examines the structure of common clinical datasets in depth, focusing on the MIMIC-III database. Understanding the schema, data types, and limitations of a real EHR dataset is a prerequisite for building meaningful ML models on clinical data.

## MIMIC-III Overview

MIMIC-III (Medical Information Mart for Intensive Care) is a freely available EHR database from Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center, containing data from over 40,000 ICU patients. It includes demographics, vital signs, lab results, medications, notes, imaging reports, and mortality outcomes. It is one of the most widely used datasets in clinical ML research.

MIMIC-III has an "ADMISSIONS" table and a "PATIENTS" table. What is the difference in granularity, and why does this matter when constructing a cohort?

<showhide>
The PATIENTS table has one row per unique patient (identified by SUBJECT_ID). The ADMISSIONS table has one row per hospital admission (identified by HADM_ID), and a single patient can have multiple admissions. When constructing a cohort, it is important not to confuse the unit of analysis: if you want one row per patient, you may need to aggregate or select admissions. If you inadvertently treat multiple admissions from the same patient as independent, you introduce data leakage and violate the independence assumption.

## Phenotyping

*Phenotyping* is the process of identifying patients with a specific condition or characteristic from EHR data. This can be done using ICD codes (simple rule-based), NLP on notes, or ML (e.g., logistic regression trained on hand-labeled cases). The quality of a phenotyping algorithm affects the quality of downstream tasks.

<checkyourself>
What is the difference between a *prevalent* and an *incident* case in cohort construction, and why does it matter for prediction tasks?

<showhide>
A *prevalent* case is a patient who already has a condition at the time of cohort entry. An *incident* case is a patient who develops the condition after cohort entry (new onset). For prediction tasks, using prevalent cases as positive labels can cause bias: if you're trying to predict who will develop a disease, you should only include patients who didn't have it at baseline (i.e., at risk). Including prevalent cases inflates the apparent performance of simple features like age and prior diagnoses that track long-standing disease rather than predicting new onset.
</showhide>
</checkyourself>
