## Survival Analysis

Many clinical outcomes are best modeled as *time-to-event* rather than binary outcomes. A patient who dies at day 31 is different from one who lives for years, yet both would be labeled "negative" by a 30-day binary outcome. Survival analysis methods (Kaplan-Meier, Cox proportional hazards, deep survival models) handle *censored* observations — patients who left the study before the event was observed.

<checkyourself>
What does it mean for an observation to be *right-censored* in a survival analysis context, and why can't you simply exclude censored patients?

<showhide>
A right-censored observation is one where we know the patient survived at least until time $t$ (when they left the study, were lost to follow-up, or the study ended), but we do not know their eventual event time. Excluding censored patients would introduce selection bias: censored patients are often systematically different (they may have left because they were healthier, or the study ended before long survivors experienced the event). Survival analysis methods like the Kaplan-Meier estimator and Cox model use the partial information from censored observations (knowing the patient survived until at least time $t$) rather than discarding it.
</showhide>

What is the difference between *internal validation* (train/test split from the same dataset) and *external validation* (testing on a different hospital's data)? Why does external validation provide a stronger test of model generalizability?

<showhide>
Internal validation splits data from the same source (e.g., random 80/20 split of MIMIC-III). This tests whether the model generalizes to held-out patients from the same distribution. External validation tests the model on data from a different institution, time period, or population — a much more demanding test. A model that performs well internally but poorly externally has learned institution-specific patterns (local coding habits, patient mix, care protocols) that do not generalize. External validation is the gold standard for assessing clinical deployability.
