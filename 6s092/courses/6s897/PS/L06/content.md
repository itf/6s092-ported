# Physiological Time-Series

Physiological time-series data — such as ECG waveforms, continuous blood pressure, SpO2, and EEG — present unique modeling challenges: high sampling rates, irregular timestamps, multivariate correlations, and clinical noise. This lecture covers approaches to modeling such data for tasks like arrhythmia detection, early warning systems, and sleep staging.

## Characteristics of Physiological Signals

Unlike tabular EHR data recorded hourly or daily, physiological waveforms are sampled at hundreds or thousands of Hz. A 10-second ECG at 500 Hz produces 5,000 data points per lead, across 12 leads. Feature extraction (QRS detection, heart rate variability) reduces dimensionality but may discard discriminative information that raw deep learning can exploit.

## Deep Learning on ECG

Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) applied directly to raw ECG waveforms have achieved cardiologist-level performance on arrhythmia detection. The CNN learns to detect relevant waveform morphologies (P wave, QRS complex, T wave) without hand-engineered feature extraction. Key architectural choices include 1D convolutions, residual connections, and careful handling of the sampling rate.

## Handling Irregular Sampling and Missing Data

ICU physiological data is often irregularly sampled — a blood pressure measurement may be taken every 5 minutes when the patient is stable, but every minute during a crisis. Standard RNNs assume fixed time steps. Adaptations include: interpolation to a regular grid, adding elapsed time as a feature, or using ODE-based continuous-time models (Neural ODEs).

<checkyourself>
Describe the main tradeoff between using raw waveforms vs. hand-engineered features (like HRV metrics) as input to a clinical ML model.

<showhide>
Raw waveforms allow the model to discover features automatically without domain knowledge, potentially capturing subtle patterns that hand-engineering misses. However, they require much more data to train, are computationally expensive, and are harder to interpret. Hand-engineered features encode domain knowledge (e.g., QRS width, HRV), are more data-efficient, more interpretable, and easier to validate clinically. In practice, raw waveform deep learning outperforms hand-engineering when large labeled datasets are available (e.g., 100,000+ ECGs), but feature engineering remains competitive and preferable in smaller-data regimes.
</showhide>
