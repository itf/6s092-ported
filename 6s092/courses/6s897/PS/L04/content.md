# Risk Stratification, Part 1

Risk stratification is the process of assigning patients to risk categories — typically to prioritize interventions for high-risk individuals. Common examples include predicting 30-day readmission, ICU mortality, sepsis onset, and deterioration events. This lecture introduces the problem formulation, evaluation metrics, and baseline approaches.

## Problem Formulation

Risk stratification can be framed as a binary classification problem: given features extracted from a patient's record up to some index time, predict whether an outcome will occur within a future time window. The choice of outcome, time window, and patient population all affect what the model learns and how it should be evaluated.

## AUROC and AUPRC

The **Area Under the ROC Curve (AUROC)** measures the probability that a model ranks a random positive example higher than a random negative example. An AUROC of 0.5 is random; 1.0 is perfect. The **Area Under the Precision-Recall Curve (AUPRC)** is more informative when the positive class is rare, since it focuses on the model's performance on positives.

<checkyourself>
For a task where only 1% of patients have the outcome of interest, explain why AUPRC may be more informative than AUROC.

<showhide>
AUROC measures rank-ordering across all threshold values. With only 1% positives, even a model that identifies just a few true positives can achieve a high AUROC because it correctly ranks most negative pairs. AUPRC, however, penalizes the model for predicting many false positives (low precision). Since clinicians can only act on a limited number of alerts, precision among those flagged is critical. A high AUROC but low AUPRC means the model ranks positives somewhat higher than negatives but requires a very low threshold (many false alarms) to catch most true positives — clinically impractical.

## Logistic Regression Baseline

Logistic regression is a strong baseline for risk stratification. With appropriate feature engineering (bag-of-codes, lab summary statistics, vital sign aggregates), logistic regression often performs competitively with complex models. The linear model also has the advantage of interpretability and calibration.
