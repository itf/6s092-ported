# Introduction: What Makes Healthcare Unique?

Healthcare is a domain that presents unique challenges for machine learning. Unlike many other application areas, healthcare data is noisy, incomplete, high-dimensional, and subject to strict privacy regulations. Clinical outcomes are influenced by complex interactions between biology, behavior, and environment. Crucially, errors can have life-or-death consequences, raising the bar for reliability and interpretability.

## The Healthcare Data Landscape

Electronic Health Records (EHRs) contain a wealth of information: diagnoses (ICD codes), medications (RxNorm), procedures (CPT codes), lab results, vital signs, clinical notes, and imaging. However, this data is recorded for billing rather than research purposes, leading to systematic biases and missing data challenges.

<checkyourself>
Why might a diagnosis code appearing in a patient's EHR *not* mean the patient truly has that condition, and vice versa?

<showhide>
Diagnosis codes are entered for billing reasons. A code may appear because the physician ruled it *out* (documented as a differential), or because it was incorrectly coded. Conversely, a condition may be present but never formally coded if the patient never sought care, or if the diagnosis was made at a different institution whose records are not shared. This leads to both false positives and false negatives in the labeled data.
</showhide>
</checkyourself>

## Why ML in Healthcare Is Hard

Standard ML assumptions often break down in healthcare: data is not i.i.d. (patients cluster by hospital, region, time period), labels are noisy or proxy-based, and distribution shift is common as clinical practice evolves. Additionally, high-stakes decisions demand not just accuracy but also fairness, interpretability, and calibration.

<checkyourself>
A hospital wants to use an ML model to prioritize high-risk patients for outreach. Should they care more about the model's AUROC or its calibration? Why might both matter?

<showhide>
AUROC measures the model's ability to rank patients by risk (discrimination), which matters for identifying who is highest risk. Calibration matters when the actual predicted probability is used to communicate risk to clinicians or set decision thresholds — a model that says "30% risk" should really mean roughly 30% of such patients will experience the event. For a fixed ranking task (top-K outreach), AUROC suffices; for absolute risk communication, calibration is essential. Ideally, a deployed model has both good discrimination and good calibration.
</showhide>
</checkyourself>
