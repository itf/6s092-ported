# Overview of Clinical Care

Understanding how clinical care is organized is essential for building useful ML systems. Care is delivered across multiple settings — outpatient clinics, emergency departments, inpatient wards, ICUs, and long-term care facilities — each with distinct data characteristics. The clinical workflow involves assessment, diagnosis, treatment planning, and monitoring, and ML can potentially assist at each step.

## The Clinical Workflow

Physicians gather information through history-taking, physical examination, and diagnostic tests, then synthesize this into a diagnosis and treatment plan. This iterative, evidence-gathering process generates the data captured in EHRs. Understanding the *reason* data was collected helps interpret its meaning.

A patient is admitted to the hospital and has a serum sodium measurement recorded on day 1, but not on days 2–4, then again on day 5. What are two different reasons this pattern might occur?

<showhide>
(1) The physician may have checked sodium on admission and then again when suspecting an abnormality — so the *absence* of a measurement on days 2–4 might be informative (the physician did not think it was necessary to recheck). (2) The measurement may have been taken but not entered into the EHR system being queried (e.g., due to system issues or because results were recorded in a different system). The first reason makes missingness "informative" — a phenomenon called *missing not at random (MNAR)*.

## Clinical Data Types

EHRs contain structured data (lab values, vitals, coded diagnoses) and unstructured data (free-text notes). Structured data is easier to work with but captures less clinical nuance. Notes contain rich information but require NLP. Medical ontologies like SNOMED-CT, ICD-10, and LOINC provide standardized vocabularies.

## Informative Missingness

In clinical data, the fact that a measurement is *missing* often carries information. A physician orders a test because they suspect something is wrong. If a test was not ordered, that may indicate the physician thought it was unnecessary — implying the patient appeared healthy in that dimension. Models that treat missing values as random noise ignore this signal.

<checkyourself>
Describe one strategy for incorporating informative missingness into a machine learning model.

<showhide>
One common strategy is to add a binary "missingness indicator" feature for each variable: a feature $m_j = 1$ if feature $x_j$ was not measured, and $m_j = 0$ if it was measured. The original feature $x_j$ is then imputed (e.g., with the mean or median) for missing entries, while $m_j$ allows the model to learn a different pattern for missing vs. observed values. This is simple and effective in practice. More sophisticated approaches include multiple imputation or using models (like RNNs) that naturally handle variable-length, irregularly sampled sequences.
