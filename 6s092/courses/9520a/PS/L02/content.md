# L02: The Learning Problem in Perspective

This lecture situates the learning problem within a broader mathematical and statistical framework. The key concepts are: the target function or distribution, different loss functions for regression and classification, the expected risk (true risk) versus empirical risk, and the fundamental tradeoff between bias and variance. The Bayes optimal predictor minimizes expected risk and serves as the theoretical gold standard.

## Expected Risk and Empirical Risk

The **expected risk** (true risk) of a function $f$ under loss $V$ and data distribution $P(x,y)$ is:
$$R[f] = \int V(f(x), y) \, dP(x,y)$$
Since $P$ is unknown, we approximate it with the **empirical risk** over $n$ training samples:
$$R_{emp}[f] = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n V(f(x_i), y_i)$$

<checkyourself>
For the squared loss $V(f(x), y) = (f(x) - y)^2$, what is the Bayes optimal predictor $f^*(x)$?

<showhide>
For squared loss, the Bayes optimal predictor is the conditional expectation: $f^*(x) = \mathbb{E}[y \mid x]$. This minimizes the expected risk $R[f] = \mathbb{E}[(f(x)-y)^2]$ because for any fixed $x$, the minimum of $\mathbb{E}[(c - y)^2]$ over constants $c$ is $c = \mathbb{E}[y \mid x]$.
</showhide>
</checkyourself>

## Loss Functions

Different tasks call for different loss functions. For regression, squared loss $(f(x)-y)^2$ is most common. For classification with $y \in \{-1,+1\}$, the 0-1 loss $\mathbf{1}[f(x) \neq y]$ is natural but non-convex and hard to optimize, so surrogates like hinge loss or logistic loss are used.

<checkyourself>
What is the difference between the approximation error and the estimation error in statistical learning?

<showhide>
The **approximation error** (bias) is $\inf_{f \in \mathcal{H}} R[f] - R[f^*]$, the gap between the best function in $\mathcal{H}$ and the true Bayes optimal $f^*$. It measures how well the hypothesis class can represent the truth. The **estimation error** (variance) is $R[\hat{f}] - \inf_{f \in \mathcal{H}} R[f]$, the gap between the ERM solution $\hat{f}$ and the best in class, due to finite data. The total excess risk is approximation error + estimation error.
</showhide>
</checkyourself>
