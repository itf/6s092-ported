# L06: Nonparametric Techniques and Regularization Theory

Nonparametric methods make minimal assumptions about the functional form of the target. This lecture covers **kernel density estimation (Parzen windows)**, **k-nearest neighbor (k-NN) regression**, and how these connect to regularization. As the sample size grows, nonparametric methods are shown to be consistent under mild conditions — they can approximate any continuous function given enough data.

## Kernel Density Estimation

Given i.i.d. samples $x_1, \ldots, x_n$ from density $p(x)$, the **Parzen window estimator** is:
$$\hat{p}_h(x) = \frac{1}{n h^d} \sum_{i=1}^n K\left(\frac{x - x_i}{h}\right)$$
where $K$ is a kernel function (e.g., Gaussian) and $h > 0$ is the bandwidth. This is a nonparametric density estimator.

<checkyourself>
What is the bias-variance tradeoff for kernel density estimation as $h$ changes?

<showhide>
- **Small $h$**: The estimator is highly local (low bias, captures fine structure) but noisy (high variance, wiggly). Each data point creates a sharp peak.
- **Large $h$**: The estimator is smooth (low variance) but may miss fine structure in the density (high bias). The estimate is over-smoothed.
- The optimal bandwidth minimizes mean squared error and typically scales as $h \sim n^{-1/(d+4)}$ for $d$-dimensional data.
</showhide>
</checkyourself>

## k-Nearest Neighbor Regression

In **k-NN regression**, the prediction at $x$ is the average of the $k$ nearest training outputs:
$$\hat{f}(x) = \frac{1}{k} \sum_{i \in N_k(x)} y_i$$
where $N_k(x)$ is the set of $k$ training points closest to $x$. As $n \to \infty$ with $k \to \infty$ and $k/n \to 0$, k-NN is consistent.

How does the connection between kernel regression and regularization theory manifest?

<showhide>
Kernel regression can be viewed as an approximation to regularized least squares. In regularization theory, the solution $f^*(x) = \sum_i c_i K(x, x_i)$ uses a reproducing kernel. For large $n$ and appropriate scaling, the Nadaraya-Watson estimator approximates the solution of a regularized problem where the regularizer favors smooth functions in the RKHS defined by $K$.
</showhide>
